Internal linking

Shopify Internal Linking: A Simple SEO Guide

By Ollie Hermans|18 September 2025

Internal links help shoppers discover the next useful page and help search engines understand how products, collections, and articles relate to one another.

What internal linking does for a Shopify store

An internal link connects one page on a store to another page on the same domain. Navigation menus and breadcrumbs are internal links, but merchants can also add contextual links inside blog posts, collection descriptions, product copy, FAQs, and other pages.

For shoppers, a useful link answers what next. A care guide can point to the product it explains. A buying guide can lead to the appropriate collection. A product page can link to detailed sizing or installation advice. These paths reduce the work required to find relevant information.

For search engines, links provide discovery paths and context about relationships between pages. A logical network of links makes it easier to see that several guides, products, and collections belong to the same topic. Internal linking is not a shortcut to rankings, but it is a practical part of a clear site structure.

Build simple product and topic clusters

Start with the store's main collections and the customer questions attached to them. Treat a collection or comprehensive guide as the centre of a small topic cluster, then connect supporting articles that cover selection, use, care, comparison, and troubleshooting.

A running store might have a trail shoes collection supported by guides about choosing grip, measuring fit, cleaning muddy shoes, and deciding when to replace them. Each guide can link to the collection and to another article where it is genuinely helpful. The collection can also link back to the strongest evergreen guidance.

Keep the map understandable. If a link exists only because two pages share a keyword, it may not help a reader. Link pages because the destination adds information or offers a sensible next action.

  • Choose one important collection, product group, or customer problem
  • List the distinct questions people ask around it
  • Match each question to an existing or planned page
  • Connect the pages where a reader would benefit from continuing

Use anchor text that describes the destination

Anchor text is the clickable wording of a link. It should give readers a reasonable expectation of the page they will open. Descriptive phrases such as merino wool care guide or waterproof hiking jackets are more useful than click here, learn more, or a pasted web address.

Keep the wording natural in the sentence and concise enough to scan. It does not need to repeat the destination page's exact title, and every link to the same page does not need identical wording. Avoid turning whole paragraphs into links or packing several keyword-heavy links into one sentence.

The surrounding context matters too. A descriptive link in a relevant paragraph is clearer than the same link dropped into an unrelated footer list.

Add links in both useful directions

New blog posts often link to products, but older pages should also help readers discover the new article. After publishing, identify two or three relevant established posts, collections, or pages and add a contextual link where the new guide improves the journey.

This habit prevents useful content from becoming orphaned, meaning it has no meaningful internal links pointing to it. A page can appear in a sitemap and still be difficult for customers to encounter through normal browsing. Linking from an established relevant page gives it a real place in the site structure.

Also connect related articles laterally. A guide to choosing a coffee grinder can naturally lead to an article about grind size. The second article can link back when choosing equipment is a useful prerequisite.

Avoid common internal linking mistakes

More links are not automatically better. Repeated site-wide links, unrelated product blocks, and long lists of destinations can distract shoppers without adding context. Give priority to the pages that best answer the next question.

Check that links use normal crawlable HTML and point directly to the intended canonical URL. Avoid chains of redirects where the destination has moved more than once. If a product is permanently removed, update the surrounding sentence as well as the URL so the recommendation remains accurate.

Do not create multiple near-identical articles and rely on links to separate them. If two pages satisfy the same search need, consolidate or clarify their roles. A clean content structure is easier to link and easier for readers to understand.

  • Vague anchor text such as click here
  • Links to unrelated products added only for promotion
  • Broken links and unnecessary redirect chains
  • New posts with no links from established pages
  • Several articles competing to answer the same question

Run a lightweight internal link review

Review internal links whenever a product range changes and as part of a quarterly content check. Begin with priority collections and the blog posts receiving organic impressions. Confirm that every important article has at least one relevant route from another page and offers a sensible next step of its own.

Look for broken destinations, outdated product recommendations, generic anchor text, orphaned posts, and pages with so many links that the main path is unclear. Search Console and analytics can show which articles attract visitors and where those readers continue, while a crawler can help inventory links at larger scale.

Finish by adding a few high-value links, not hundreds of mechanical ones. A maintained structure that reflects the current catalogue and customer journey is more useful than a one-time linking project.

  • Check priority pages for relevant incoming links
  • Repair broken or redirected destinations
  • Update links affected by catalogue changes
  • Replace vague anchors with descriptive wording
  • Add links between new articles and established related pages

Common questions

What is an internal link in Shopify?

An internal link points from one page on your Shopify store to another page on the same store, such as a blog post linking to a product, collection, or related guide.

How many internal links should a blog post contain?

There is no useful fixed number. Add links where they genuinely help a reader understand the topic or continue their journey, and avoid repetitive or unrelated links.

What anchor text should I use for internal links?

Use short, natural wording that describes the destination, such as 'winter running collection' instead of vague phrases such as 'click here'.
